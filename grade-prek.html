Phase One · Pre-K

The Most Important Year in the System

Not because of what is taught — but because of what is built.

Every critical thinking skill this curriculum develops — Toulmin mapping, predicate logic, Red Team inoculation, policy reasoning — depends on a student who can sit with uncertainty, notice their own reactions, and ask questions without shame. None of those capacities arrive automatically. They are built, slowly, in early childhood.

Pre-K is where the Internal Heatmap is seeded. Not as a named concept — but as a lived practice. A child who learns at age four that "I feel angry right now" is information, not an emergency, is a child who will be able to keep their Logic Gate open at age fourteen when a manipulative headline triggers the same response.

There are no tracks in Pre-K. No assessments. No competency targets in the academic sense. The goal is a child who is curious, regulated, and ready to learn.

No tracks. No sorting. No specialization. Every child does everything. The only goal is a human foundation sturdy enough to build on.

Core Domain · Emotional Development

Feeling Is Information

The earliest form of the Internal Heatmap.

What Students Practice

  • Naming feelings with precision: anger, frustration, excitement, fear, sadness, confusion, pride
  • 'I notice I feel upset. That is a signal, not a verdict.' — practiced daily in morning circle
  • The body responds before the mind catches up — normalizing that experience removes shame from it
  • Conflict resolution through words: naming what happened, naming what was felt, naming what is needed
  • Recognizing that two people can feel differently about the same event — perspective as a concept introduced through play

🌡 The Heatmap Seed — Age 4

This is where the vertical thread begins. The Internal Heatmap that an 8th grader uses to evaluate a disinformation campaign starts here — with a 4-year-old learning to say 'I feel scared' instead of running away or hitting.

Teachers use a simple feelings chart and a daily circle check-in. 'How does your body feel right now?' is asked every morning — not as therapy, but as practice. The goal is automaticity: noticing before reacting.

By the end of Pre-K, a child who can say 'I feel frustrated because I couldn't build the tower' has performed the same cognitive move that will later be called the Logic Gate Protocol.

Core Domain · Physical Development

The Body Teaches the Mind

Spatial reasoning, fine motor control, and embodied logic.

What Students Practice

  • Fine motor skills through drawing, cutting, building, threading, and clay work
  • Gross motor skills through structured movement, dance, obstacle courses, and outdoor play
  • Spatial reasoning through block construction: What happens if I put the big block on top of the small one?
  • Cause-and-effect through physical action: push/pull, stack/topple, fill/empty
  • Pattern recognition through physical arrangement: sorting by color, size, shape, and sequence

Physical play is not a break from learning — it is the learning. Block construction is spatial reasoning. Sorting by color is categorical logic. A child who builds a stable tower has solved a structural engineering problem.

Core Domain · Pattern & Early Logic

Patterns Before Numbers

The same cognitive muscle that will later perform Boolean logic.

What Students Practice

  • Sorting and categorizing: 'These all belong together because...'
  • Sequencing: what comes first, next, last — the earliest argument structure
  • Simple if/then through play: 'If I water the seed, then it grows'
  • Story structure: beginning, middle, end — the first argument map
  • Noticing when a sequence is broken: 'Something is wrong here'
  • Repeating patterns: ABAB, AABB — regularity as a concept
Core Domain · Curiosity

Wonder as a Practice

Questions are never disruptive. They are the point.

Classroom Protocol

  • Teachers model 'why?' and 'how do we know?' in daily conversation — not as challenges but as genuine inquiry
  • No question is treated as silly, disruptive, or off-topic. Every question is acknowledged.
  • The phrase 'I don't know — let's find out' is used freely and deliberately
  • Children are asked 'what do you notice?' before they are told what to see
  • Disagreement between children is welcomed and guided: 'That's interesting — you think X and she thinks Y. How could we find out who's right?'

A child who arrives in Kindergarten still asking 'why?' without apology is already ahead of most adults in the most important cognitive habit this curriculum teaches.

The Vertical Thread

The Heatmap Seed in Practice

What this looks like at age 4 — and why it matters at age 14.

The Internal Heatmap is not taught as a concept in Pre-K. It is built as a habit through daily practice. Here is what that looks like concretely:

Daily Practice Examples

  • Morning Circle: 'Before we start — how does your body feel right now? Show me on the feelings chart.'
  • Conflict moments: 'I can see your body is really upset. Can you tell me what the feeling is called? Good. Now — what happened?'
  • Story discussions: 'When the wolf blew the house down, how did the pig feel? How do you know? Did your body feel anything when I read that part?'
  • Transition moments: 'We're switching activities now. I notice some of you look frustrated. That's okay. Can you name it?'

🌡 The Connection to Grade 7 Inoculation

In Grade 7, students are asked to notice their physiological response to a charged headline before engaging with it analytically. That is an advanced version of exactly what a Pre-K student does when they say 'I feel angry' before they act.

The vertical thread is literal: the neural pathway trained at age 4 — notice, name, pause — is the same pathway activated at age 13 when a Red Team exercise triggers discomfort. We are not teaching a new skill in 7th grade. We are applying a deeply practiced one.

What Pre-K Builds

Not Skills — Capacities

What a Pre-K graduate carries into Kindergarten.

  • 1
    Can name at least 6 feelings accuratelyAnger, frustration, excitement, fear, sadness, and confusion — with enough precision to distinguish between them.
  • 2
    Understands that a feeling is information, not a commandThe foundational Heatmap insight: 'I feel this' is a signal to notice, not a directive to act.
  • 3
    Can sort, sequence, and identify broken patternsThe earliest form of logical structure — categorical thinking and sequence integrity.
  • 4
    Asks 'why?' and 'how do we know?' without promptingCuriosity as a cultivated habit, not a natural accident.
  • 5
    Can sit with frustration long enough to describe itEmotional regulation sufficient for sustained attention — the prerequisite for all future focused learning.
  • 6
    Understands that two people can see the same thing differentlyPerspective as a variable — the seed of the counterargument.
