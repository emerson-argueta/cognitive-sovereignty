Phase One · Kindergarten

Structure Enters the Picture

Play remains the vehicle — but now it has direction.

Kindergarten builds on the emotional foundation of Pre-K by introducing the first explicit logical structures: cause-and-effect, sequencing, and the basic claim-and-reason format. These arrive through story, play, and conversation — never through drill.

The most important linguistic distinction of the year is 'because' versus 'so': understanding that these words point in opposite directions — one toward explanation, one toward consequence — is the earliest form of directional logic that will later become predicate reasoning.

The goal is not a child who can recite facts. It is a child who asks 'why did that happen?' and waits for a real answer.

Core Domain

Story as the First Logic Map

Beginning, middle, end — the earliest argument structure.

What Students Practice

  • Identifying beginning, middle, and end in stories — sequence as logical structure
  • Cause and effect in narratives: 'The pig was afraid because the wolf blew the house down'
  • 'Because' vs 'so': 'She was cold because it was winter' vs 'It was winter, so she was cold'
  • Predicting what comes next — hypothesis formation through story
  • Identifying when a story 'doesn't make sense' and articulating why
  • Retelling a story in sequence — the first argument summary
Core Domain · Emotional Logic

Connecting Feelings to Decisions

Feelings have causes. So do decisions. Both can be traced.

What Students Practice

  • 'She did that because she felt...' — linking character behavior to emotional state
  • The same event can make different people feel different things — perspective as a logical variable
  • Noticing their own feeling before reacting — the Heatmap practice continues from Pre-K
  • Drawing a 'feeling map' of a story: where did the character's emotion change, and why?
  • Distinguishing between 'I feel' and 'I want' — separating state from demand

🌡 The Feeling Map — Kindergarten Version

Students draw simple timelines of a story's emotional arc: happy → scared → relieved. This is their first visualization exercise and their first heatmap simultaneously.

The teacher asks: 'When you heard that part, did YOUR body feel anything?' This connects the character's heatmap to the student's own — the first step toward the interoception practice that defines the full curriculum.

Core Domain · Pattern Recognition

Patterns in Everything

From beads on a string to the rhythm of a story.

What Students Practice

  • Visual and physical patterns: ABAB, AABB, ABCABC — identifying, extending, and creating
  • Sequences with missing elements: 'What comes next?'
  • Patterns in stories: repeated phrases, recurring events, predictable structures
  • Simple if/then through daily routine: 'If it rains, then we stay inside'
  • Categorizing by multiple attributes: 'These are big AND red'
Core Domain · Visualization

Drawing What a Story Means

Not what it looks like — what it means.

What Students Practice

  • Drawing cause-and-effect pairs: the cause on the left, the effect on the right, an arrow between them
  • Before/after diagrams: what did the character want, what did they do, what happened?
  • Feeling maps: a simple timeline of a character's emotional journey through a story
  • Building physical models of story events using blocks or clay

When a Kindergartner draws an arrow from 'pig scared' to 'pig builds stronger house,' they are creating their first argument diagram. The Toulmin Flowchart in 7th grade is the same cognitive act at higher resolution.

What Kindergarten Builds

Ready for Grade 1

The capacities a Kindergarten graduate carries forward.

  • 1
    Can identify cause and effect in a storyAnd can distinguish between 'because' and 'so' — directional logic in natural language.
  • 2
    Can retell a story in sequenceBeginning, middle, end — with the key causal events in the right order.
  • 3
    Can draw a simple cause-and-effect diagramTwo boxes and an arrow: the earliest argument map.
  • 4
    Can say 'I think X because Y'The simplest form of claim-and-reason — the foundation of every argument structure that follows.
  • 5
    Recognizes that the same event affects people differentlyPerspective is a variable. This insight is the prerequisite for counterargument.
  • 6
    Continues to ask 'why?' without promptingCuriosity remains a practiced habit, not a natural accident.
