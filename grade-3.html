Phase One · Grade 3

Where Did That Come From?

The claim-reason-evidence structure now includes a fourth question: who said it?

Every piece of information has an origin. Grade 3 teaches students to trace claims back to their source and ask three questions: Who made this claim? What did they want? What evidence did they provide? These are the questions that, by 8th grade, will become a full credibility evaluation protocol.

Truth tables arrive this year — not as abstract math, but as a visual tool for tracking when AND/OR/NOT conditions are satisfied. Students discover that logic can be drawn, not just spoken.

Core Domain · Source Evaluation

Who Said It and Why

The three questions that begin every source evaluation.

What Students Practice

  • The three source questions: Who wrote this? Why did they write it? What evidence do they give?
  • Primary vs secondary sources: the difference between the original claim and a report of it
  • Recognizing bias in simple terms: 'This company is saying their own product is the best — is that surprising?'
  • Comparing two sources on the same topic: what do they agree on? What do they disagree on?
  • The question 'how do we know?' applied to every claim encountered in reading

The goal is not skepticism of everything — it is the habit of checking. 'I wonder where that came from' is the sentence this year is designed to make automatic.

Core Domain · Fallacies

Four Fallacies This Year

Two carried from Grade 2, two new ones introduced through story.

Carried From Grade 2 (deepened)

  • Appeal to Popularity: Now applied to more complex examples — social media trends, peer pressure in stories
  • Ad Hominem: Distinguishing personal attacks from legitimate challenges to credibility

New This Year

  • Strawman: Introduced through a story where a character misrepresents what their friend said — 'That's not what I meant!' The lesson: attack the real argument, not a weaker version of it
  • False Dichotomy: 'You're either with me or against me' — the story explores the middle ground that the false choice erases. Students map the full spectrum of options.
Core Domain · Logic

Truth Tables & Logic Gates

Boolean logic becomes visual — and verifiable.

What Students Practice

  • Simple truth tables: two inputs (A and B), one output — AND, OR, NOT
  • Filling in a truth table: systematically testing all possible combinations
  • Logic gate symbols introduced: AND gate (D-shape), OR gate (curved), NOT gate (triangle with bubble)
  • Drawing a simple logic circuit for a two-condition rule
  • Real-world application: 'The door opens if card is valid AND time is between 8am and 5pm' — draw this as a gate
Core Domain · Visualization

Argument Maps With Sources

Adding a source layer to the claim-reason-evidence diagram.

What Students Practice

  • Extending the Grade 1/2 argument map to include a source box: where did this evidence come from?
  • Simple flowcharts: question → search → evaluate source → accept or reject → update claim
  • Side-by-side source comparison: two boxes, same claim, different sources — what's different?
  • Logical connector map: claims connected by 'because,' 'therefore,' 'however,' and 'but'
The Vertical Thread

Heatmap + Source Bias

Connecting the emotional response to where a claim came from.

🌡 New Layer: Source Bias and the Body

This year the Heatmap practice expands: students notice not just their response to a claim, but their response to who made the claim. Do you trust a doctor more than a random person? Do you distrust a company when it praises itself?

The Thinking Journal prompt: 'This week I noticed I trusted ___ more than ___ because ___. Was that fair?'

This is the first time students connect the physiological trust response — 'this person seems credible' — to the question of whether that response is justified by evidence.

What Grade 3 Builds

Ready for Grade 4

  • 1
    Can evaluate a source using three questionsWho wrote it, why they wrote it, and what evidence they provide — applied consistently.
  • 2
    Recognizes four fallacies by patternAppeal to Popularity, Ad Hominem, Strawman, and False Dichotomy — spotted in stories and real conversation.
  • 3
    Can fill in a truth table for AND, OR, NOTBoolean logic made visual — systematic and verifiable.
  • 4
    Can draw an argument map including a source layerThe evidence now has an origin that is evaluated alongside the claim itself.
  • 5
    Notices trust responses and questions them'I automatically trusted that — is my trust justified?' as a practiced reflex.
