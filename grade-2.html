Phase One · Grade 2

The First Line in the Sand

Some things can be checked. Some things are preferences. And some people try to make preferences look like facts.

The fact/opinion distinction is the first epistemic line this curriculum draws explicitly. It arrives not as a grammar lesson but as a power tool — students learn it because it helps them catch adults (and themselves) doing something slippery.

Fallacies enter this year as named patterns in stories and conversations, not as abstract definitions. 'Everyone says so' is the first one — because it is everywhere, and students already know it feels wrong before they have a name for it.

Core Domain · Reasoning

What Can Be Checked

Facts have evidence. Opinions have preferences. Some claims disguise one as the other.

What Students Practice

  • Sorting statements: 'Dogs are better than cats' vs 'Dogs are mammals' — which can be checked?
  • The question: 'How would we find out if that's true?' applied to every claim
  • Loaded language: words that sneak an opinion into a description ('obviously,' 'everyone knows,' 'clearly')
  • Spotting when an opinion is stated as if it were a fact — and naming it
  • Writing both: an opinion sentence and a fact sentence about the same topic
Core Domain · First Fallacies

Naming the Tricks

Two fallacies introduced this year — as story patterns, not Latin terms.

Appeal to Popularity — 'Everyone Says So'

  • Introduced through a story: the whole class says the moon is made of cheese — does that make it true?
  • The question: 'Does it matter how many people believe something if there's no evidence?'
  • Students find examples in their own experience: 'Everyone has this toy so I need it'
  • Written practice: finding the hidden claim and asking 'but how do we know?'

Ad Hominem — Attacking the Person, Not the Idea

  • Introduced through a story: 'Don't listen to him — he's weird'
  • The key question: 'Is the argument bad, or is the person who made it bad?'
  • Practice: separating the speaker from the claim — evaluating the argument on its own
  • Connection to the Heatmap: 'Does your feeling about a person change how you hear their argument?'

Fallacies are introduced as patterns to recognize, not vocabulary to memorize. A student who can spot 'everyone says so' in a conversation has learned something — a student who can spell 'ad populum' has not necessarily learned anything.

Core Domain · Pattern & Logic

AND, OR, NOT — The First Logic Operators

Introduced through sorting games before they are named as logic.

What Students Practice

  • AND: Sorting blocks that are big AND red — both conditions must be true
  • OR: Sorting blocks that are big OR red — at least one condition must be true
  • NOT: 'All animals that are NOT dogs' — exclusion as a logical operation
  • Combining operators: 'Find something that is big AND red AND NOT round'
  • Verbal puzzles: 'I'm thinking of a number that is greater than 5 AND less than 10 AND NOT 7'
  • Introduction to the truth table — a simple two-column chart showing when a rule is satisfied
Core Domain · Visualization

Venn Diagrams & Evidence Charts

The first formal visual logic tools.

What Students Practice

  • Venn diagrams with two circles: things that are true of A, things true of B, things true of both
  • Evidence charts: the claim in the middle, evidence items around it, lines connecting them
  • Simple yes/no decision tree: 'Is this a fact? → Can we check it? → Where would we look?'
  • Fact vs opinion sorting visual: two labeled columns, statements sorted into each
What Grade 2 Builds

Ready for Grade 3

  • 1
    Can distinguish facts from opinions reliablyAnd can identify when an opinion is disguised as a fact through loaded language.
  • 2
    Recognizes two fallacies by patternAppeal to Popularity ('everyone says so') and Ad Hominem (attacking the speaker, not the argument).
  • 3
    Can apply AND, OR, NOT to sorting problemsBoolean logic as a physical and verbal practice before it becomes symbolic.
  • 4
    Can build a Venn diagram to compare two thingsThe first formal visualization tool — used to organize claims and evidence.
  • 5
    Asks 'how would we check that?' automaticallyThe epistemic reflex is now consistent — not just when prompted.
  • 6
    Maintains the Thinking Journal — including fallacy sightingsStudents begin recording real examples of fallacies they spot in daily life.
