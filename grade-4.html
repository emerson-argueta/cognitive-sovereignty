Phase One · Grade 4

Data Is Not Proof

It is evidence. And evidence must be interpreted — which is where most errors happen.

Grade 4 introduces a fundamental distinction: a graph that shows two things moving together does not prove that one caused the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates rise together every summer. That does not mean ice cream causes drowning. Correlation is a pattern. Causation is a mechanism. Finding one does not establish the other.

Argument maps grow a new feature this year: branches. A branching flowchart shows not just a single chain of reasoning but the points where counterarguments enter — and how they are addressed.

Core Domain · Data

Reading Data Honestly

What charts show, what they hide, and how to tell the difference.

What Students Practice

  • Reading bar charts, line graphs, and pie charts — what question does each type answer best?
  • Identifying what a chart shows AND what it doesn't show
  • Truncated axes: a graph that starts at 90% instead of 0% makes a small difference look huge
  • Sample size: '3 out of 4 dentists' — but how many dentists were asked?
  • The question: 'What would I need to know to trust this data?'
Core Domain · Logic

Correlation ≠ Causation

The most common error in everyday reasoning about data.

What Students Practice

  • The ice cream / drowning example — what's the hidden third variable? (Summer)
  • Generating their own silly correlations: shoe size and reading ability in children (both increase with age)
  • The three conditions for causation: correlation, correct time order, and no better explanation
  • Identifying alternative explanations for correlations in news headlines
  • Writing: 'This data shows ___ and ___ are correlated. This might be caused by ___. But it could also be caused by ___.'

By the end of this unit, students should be permanently suspicious of headlines that say 'X causes Y' based on a single study. That suspicion is one of the most useful cognitive tools a person can have.

Core Domain · Fallacies

Five Fallacies Recognized

Adding Slippery Slope and Anecdotal Evidence to the set.

New This Year

  • Slippery Slope: 'If we allow X, then Y will happen, then Z, then everything falls apart.' Students map the chain and ask: does each step actually lead to the next? Where is the real evidence for each link?
  • Anecdotal Evidence: 'My grandmother smoked and lived to 90.' One example is not a pattern. Students learn to ask: 'How many cases would we need to see before this counts as evidence?'
Core Domain · Visualization

Argument Flowcharts With Branches

The argument map gains the ability to handle disagreement.

What Students Practice

  • Adding a counterargument branch to the argument map: the main claim, the objection, the response
  • Decision flowcharts with multiple conditions and outcomes
  • Drawing the Slippery Slope as a chain — and identifying which links are broken
  • Correlation vs causation diagram: two parallel tracks with a 'hidden variable?' box between them
Core Domain · Writing

Counterargument — First Appearance

A strong argument does not pretend the other side doesn't exist.

What Students Practice

  • Identifying the strongest objection to their own claim — not the weakest one
  • Writing a concession sentence: 'It is true that ___, however ___'
  • The structure: Claim → Reason → Evidence → Counterargument → Response to counterargument
  • The question: 'What would someone who disagrees with me say? Is their point fair?'
What Grade 4 Builds

Ready for Grade 5

  • 1
    Can distinguish correlation from causationAnd can identify the third-variable explanation for spurious correlations.
  • 2
    Can read and critically evaluate charts and graphsIncluding identifying truncated axes, small sample sizes, and missing context.
  • 3
    Recognizes five fallacies by patternNow including Slippery Slope and Anecdotal Evidence.
  • 4
    Can draw an argument flowchart with a counterargument branchThe argument structure now accommodates disagreement.
  • 5
    Writes a counterargument and responseAcknowledging the opposing view and responding to it — not ignoring it.
