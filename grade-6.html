Phase Two · Grade 6

The Structure Changes

From subject-by-subject instruction to one Core Problem, five lenses, every week.

Grade 6 marks the transition from Phase One's developmental foundation to Phase Two's interdisciplinary Deep Work model. The 35/15 focus-break rhythm begins. Core Problems replace subject-isolated lessons. The Orchestrator pre-links all five lenses to each week's problem.

The Internal Heatmap — practiced since Pre-K — is now named, formalized, and taught as an explicit analytical tool. Students learn to describe what has always been a habit.

Nothing in Phase One was wasted. Every fallacy, every cause-and-effect diagram, every Thinking Journal entry — it all feeds directly into what Grade 6 formalizes.

Structural Model

The Deep Work Block

8:00 AM – 1:00 PM. One problem. Five angles. No context-switching.

Each week is anchored by a Core Problem — a real-world question that all five lenses address simultaneously. The five lenses are: Reading & Comprehension, Grammar & Writing, Pattern Recognition, Visualization, and Project Work.

LensSessionWhat Happens
Reading & ComprehensionSessions 1 & 3Analyzing real texts for bias, argument structure, and fallacies related to the Core Problem
Grammar & WritingSessions 2 & Practice 2Writing claims, arguments, and responses using the Core Problem as subject matter

The Vertical Thread

The Internal Heatmap — Formally Named

What has been practiced since Pre-K is now an explicit analytical protocol.

🌡 The Heatmap Protocol — Grade 6 Version

Students are now taught to describe their Heatmap practice in explicit terms: Before I engage with this claim, I notice what my body is doing. A spike in heart rate or a feeling of immediate agreement or outrage is a signal that a manipulation lever may have been pulled.

The Logic Gate protocol: when a strong emotional response is detected, the Logic Gate stays closed until the emotional pulse subsides. Then — and only then — are analytical tools applied.

The Thinking Journal gains a new section this year: the weekly Heatmap log. 'This week I noticed a strong response to ___. The feeling was ___. After pausing, my analysis was ___.'

Core Domain · Fallacies

Four Formal Fallacies

The six from Phase One plus four new ones — introduced in the context of Core Problems.

Carried From Phase One (Now Formally Named)

  • Appeal to Popularity (Ad Populum) · Strawman · False Dichotomy · Slippery Slope

New in Grade 6

  • Appeal to Authority (Ad Verecundiam): Citing an expert as proof rather than as one piece of evidence. 'A doctor said it' does not end the inquiry.
  • Circular Reasoning (Begging the Question): 'The Bible is true because the Bible says so.' The conclusion is hidden in the premise.
  • Hasty Generalization: Concluding from too few cases — 'I met two rude people from that city, so everyone there is rude.'
  • Post Hoc (False Cause): 'I wore my lucky socks and we won — my socks caused the win.' Sequence is not causation.
Core Domain · Visualization

Mind Maps to Logic Diagrams

The visualization progression for Phase Two begins.

What Students Practice

  • Mind Maps: central concept, branching ideas, connections — used to explore a Core Problem before analyzing it
  • Argument Diagrams: Toulmin structure (from Grade 5) now drawn consistently for every major claim
  • Bias Heatmaps: visual annotations on a text showing where emotional language is concentrated
  • Simple Boolean logic gate diagrams: AND/OR/NOT circuits drawn for real decision problems
Branching Competency

Tracks Are Introduced

Orientations, not assignments. The signal is curiosity, not test scores.

In Grade 6, students encounter all three track orientations through the Core Problem structure — but no student is assigned to a track. The Orchestrator begins recording engagement signals: where does a student linger, ask unsolicited questions, or produce work beyond the requirement?

By the end of the year, teachers initiate a conversation — not an assessment — about which kinds of problems felt most alive.

Core Competency

All Students
  • All Phase One tools applied to real-world problems
  • Four new formal fallacies recognized
  • Heatmap protocol named and practiced
  • Argument maps for every major claim

Extension

For students showing academic depth
  • Formal logic notation introduced informally
  • Extended argument maps with full rebuttal chains
  • Independent research on Core Problem topics
  • Preparation for formal Toulmin Model in Grade 7

Vocational Depth

High-intensity application
  • Core Problems applied to trade and real-world contexts
  • Boolean logic applied to practical decision rules
  • Source evaluation applied to product claims and contracts
  • Argument mapping on real workplace scenarios
