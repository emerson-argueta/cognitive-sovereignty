Phase Two · Grade 8

Research-Level Argumentation

The tools are complete. This year, students use them at full power.

Grade 8 is the culmination of Phase Two. Every tool introduced since Pre-K — the Heatmap, Boolean logic, the Toulmin Model, source evaluation, statistical reasoning — is applied at research level to complex, real-world claims.

Predicate logic extends the quantifier vocabulary ('all,' 'some,' 'none') into formal notation. Scientific skepticism provides the framework for evaluating empirical claims. And the Capstone Defense asks students to synthesize everything into a single defended argument before an external panel.

Core Domain · Logic

Predicate Logic — Quantifiers

The difference between 'some studies show' and 'all evidence confirms' is a predicate logic distinction.

What Students Practice

  • ∀ (For all): 'All swans are white' — a universal claim that one counterexample can destroy
  • ∃ (There exists): 'Some swans are black' — an existential claim that one example can prove
  • Recognizing quantifiers in natural language: 'always,' 'never,' 'sometimes,' 'most,' 'all,' 'none'
  • Spotting overgeneralization: a universal claim masquerading as a proven fact
  • The asymmetry of proof: universal claims are hard to prove (you need all cases) but easy to disprove (one counterexample). Existential claims are easy to prove (one example) but hard to disprove.
  • Formal notation practice: translating natural language claims into ∀x and ∃x notation

The most common manipulation move in political and advertising language is a universal claim stated without evidence. 'Everyone knows,' 'scientists agree,' 'studies show' — predicate logic gives students the tool to immediately ask: 'All scientists? Which studies? Show me the ∀ evidence.'

Core Domain · Scientific Skepticism

Evaluating Empirical Claims

The framework for deciding when to update a belief based on evidence.

Statistical Fallacies Mastered

  • P-hacking: Running enough tests until you find a 'significant' result by chance
  • Confidence intervals: Understanding what the error bars actually mean
  • Base rate neglect: 'This test is 99% accurate' means different things at different base rates
  • Publication bias: Studies that find nothing don't get published — the literature is skewed
  • Replication crisis: Many famous studies have failed to replicate — what should we conclude?

Cherry-Picking Deep Dive

  • Identifying which data was selected and what was omitted
  • Finding the full dataset and comparing it to the highlighted subset
  • The motivated reasoning question: 'What would someone who believed the opposite have selected?'
  • Writing a formal analysis: presenting the cherry-picked claim, the full data, and the corrected conclusion
Advanced Inoculation

Advanced Red Team Exercises

The most complex inoculation exercises of Phase Two — under mandatory Certified Facilitator supervision.

Grade 8 Red Team exercises involve sophisticated multi-layer misinformation: statistical manipulation combined with emotional framing, combined with cherry-picked sources. Students construct and then systematically dismantle these complex operations.

Certified Facilitator required. Grade 8 exercises involve the most sophisticated manipulation constructions in the Phase Two sequence. A poorly facilitated debrief at this level can produce lasting cynicism. The protocol is non-negotiable.

🌡 The Debrief Protocol — Grade 8 Version

Step 1 — Construct: Build the misinformation piece using every tool available: statistical manipulation, emotional triggers, authoritative framing, strategic omission.

Step 2 — Identify all levers: Map every manipulation technique used — which fallacy, which statistical trick, which emotional trigger, which Heatmap activation.

Step 3 — Dismantle: Systematically destroy the piece using logic tools. Every fallacy named. Every statistical manipulation reversed. Every omitted evidence restored.

Step 4 — The Anchor: 'Skepticism is the tool we use to protect the possibility of truth, not to destroy it.' The debrief ends here — always.

Year-End Assessment

The Capstone Defense

A formal presentation of an argument, a dismantled claim, or a designed system — defended before a panel.

Capstone Requirements

  • A formal written argument or claim analysis — minimum 1,500 words — following full Toulmin structure
  • A hand-drawn or digitally-produced argument map of the complete claim
  • A 15-minute oral defense before a panel that includes at least one person from outside the school
  • The panel may challenge any claim, any warrant, any source, any statistical interpretation
  • Students must respond without fallacies — challenged responses that use fallacies are noted and counted
  • A written reflection: 'What did I believe at the start of Grade 6 that I no longer believe? What changed my mind?'
Branching Competency

Track Depth in Grade 8

The same Capstone standard — three different vehicles.

Core Competency

All Students
  • Predicate logic quantifiers in natural language
  • Scientific skepticism framework applied to real claims
  • Advanced Red Team construction and dismantling
  • Full Capstone Defense with external panel

Extension

Academic depth
  • Formal predicate logic proofs in symbolic notation
  • Probability theory and statistical inference at depth
  • Philosophy of science: Popper, Kuhn, Lakatos — what counts as a scientific claim?
  • Extended Capstone with original research component

Vocational Depth

Applied track
  • Quantifier analysis applied to contract and regulatory language
  • Statistical skepticism applied to product claims, warranties, and financial terms
  • Red Team exercise using a real predatory advertising campaign
  • Capstone: a formal analysis of a real document that affects a trade or labor context
