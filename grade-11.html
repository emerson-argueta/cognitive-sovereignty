Phase Three · Grade 11

The Calling Takes Over

From guided instruction to mentored self-direction.

Grade 11 marks a structural shift: instruction gives way to mentored self-direction. The morning seminar provides context, tools, and cross-track connection. The rest of the day belongs to the student and their Capstone project.

The advisor relationship — not the teacher relationship — becomes primary. An advisor does not direct the student's work. They ask questions, surface blind spots, and ensure the work is meeting the standards the student has committed to.

A student who spends four hours in the Studio every afternoon because they can't put down their project is not being worked. They are being called. That distinction is the entire point of the Studio Model.

Structural Model

Self-Directed Work — What It Looks Like

Structure without direction. Support without control.

The Self-Directed Week

  • Students set their own weekly goals for their Capstone project at the start of each week
  • The advisor reviews goals and asks: 'What would prevent you from achieving these? What do you need?'
  • Mid-week check-in: progress against goals — self-reported, self-evaluated
  • End of week: what was completed, what changed, what was learned from what didn't work
  • Peer review sessions: other students in the track review and challenge the work
  • No grade on process — grade on demonstrated mastery at Capstone Defense
Academic Track · Grade 11

Epistemology & Original Research

What can we know, and how do we know we know it?

What Students Practice

  • Epistemology: the philosophical foundations of knowledge — rationalism, empiricism, constructivism
  • Popper, Kuhn, Lakatos: falsifiability, paradigm shifts, and research programs — how science actually progresses
  • Capstone begins: an original research question chosen by the student, methodology designed, data collection begins
  • Engaging with real academic literature: reading and critiquing actual peer-reviewed papers in the Capstone's field
  • Writing the literature review: what is already known, what is contested, where the gap is
Vocational Track · Grade 11

Full Apprenticeship

Real projects. Real clients. Real consequences.

What Students Practice

  • Minimum 3 days per week in active apprenticeship — the school day bends to accommodate the trade's schedule
  • Taking on a project with a real client and real deliverables — not a simulation
  • Advanced regulatory literacy: permits, codes, compliance documentation in the student's specific trade
  • Business reasoning: full job costing, quoting, scheduling, and margin analysis on a real project
  • Capstone begins: documentation of a complete real project from brief to delivery — every decision recorded
Engineering Track · Grade 11

Advanced Design & Failure Analysis

The build that matters — with formal accountability.

What Students Practice

  • A full-year Capstone design project: defined problem, defined constraints, iterative design, build, test, redesign
  • Advanced failure analysis: studying three to five real engineering failures at depth — reconstructing the full reasoning chain that led to each
  • Cross-disciplinary systems thinking: the student's Capstone must touch at least two systems domains (e.g., mechanical + software, agricultural + logistics)
  • Formal constraint documentation: every design decision mapped against the constraints it satisfies or violates
  • Peer design review: other engineering students formally challenge the design before build
Civic Track · Grade 11

Legal Reasoning & Advanced Policy Design

Reading case law. Constructing legal arguments. Designing defensible policy.

What Students Practice

  • Legal reasoning: how courts interpret statutes, how precedent works, how cases are argued
  • Reading and analyzing real case law in the student's area of interest
  • Advanced policy design: a real policy proposal with full evidence base, stakeholder analysis, objection response, and implementation plan
  • Defending the proposal before a real external critic with a genuine stake in the outcome
  • Applied ethics: working through real moral dilemmas in the civic domain without predetermined answers
