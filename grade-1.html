Phase One · Grade 1

The Three-Part Argument

Every formal logic structure in this curriculum is an elaboration of this.

The Toulmin Model. Predicate logic. Formal debate. These are all sophisticated versions of one simple move: making a claim, giving a reason, and pointing to evidence. Grade 1 is where that move is learned for the first time — in sentences, through stories, and in daily conversation.

The Thinking Journal is introduced this year. It is the earliest version of the metacognitive record that students will maintain through 12th grade — a private space to track what they believe, why they believe it, and what changes their mind.

Core Domain · Reasoning

Claim → Reason → Evidence

The first formal argument structure. Simple enough for a 6-year-old. Powerful enough for a Supreme Court brief.

What Students Practice

  • Claim: 'I think the bear went to sleep because it was winter.'
  • Reason: 'Bears hibernate when it gets cold.'
  • Evidence: 'The book said the forest got very cold and the bear started yawning.'
  • Distinguishing between 'I think' (claim) and 'because' (reason) in speech and writing
  • Recognizing when a reason is missing: 'She said the sky is blue, but she didn't say why she thinks that'
  • The difference between a reason and an example: a reason explains, an example shows

'I think X because Y, and I know this because Z.' If a student can say this sentence clearly and correctly about any topic, they have mastered the most fundamental unit of rational argument.

Core Domain · Emotional Logic

Feelings as the First Variable

The Heatmap practice deepens — feelings are now connected to reasoning chains.

What Students Practice

  • Why a character made a decision — tracing the emotional cause behind an action
  • Two people, same event, different feelings — and both can be right
  • Noticing their own feeling before reacting — the morning Heatmap check-in continues
  • The question: 'Did your feeling change after you found out more?'
  • Writing: 'I felt ___ because ___' — the claim-reason structure applied to emotion
Core Domain · Pattern & Logic

If/Then Enters the Room

Conditional reasoning through games, stories, and daily routine.

What Students Practice

  • If/then through daily rules: 'If it is raining, then we stay inside'
  • If/then in stories: 'If the wolf comes, then the pig will hide'
  • Completing if/then sentences: 'If I eat my lunch, then ___'
  • Finding the pattern in a sequence and identifying the rule that governs it
  • Recognizing when an if/then rule was broken — 'But it didn't happen that way — why not?'
  • Simple algorithms as ordered steps: 'First do this, then do this, then do this'
Core Domain · Visualization

Drawing the Argument

Externalizing reasoning onto paper — seeing the structure of a claim.

What Students Practice

  • Two-box diagrams: CLAIM box → REASON box, connected by an arrow labeled 'because'
  • Adding a third box: EVIDENCE — 'I know this because ___'
  • Before/after reasoning strips: the situation, the action, the result
  • Simple decision trees: 'If it rains → stay inside. If it's sunny → go outside'
  • Character emotion timelines — extending the Kindergarten feeling map
The Metacognitive Thread

The Thinking Journal — Year One

Private. Low-stakes. The beginning of a lifelong practice.

The Thinking Journal is introduced in Grade 1 as a simple notebook where students write or draw one thing they believed, one thing they noticed, or one question they had. It is never graded. It is never read aloud without the student's permission. It is private.

Weekly Prompts (Age-Appropriate)

  • 'Draw something you learned this week that surprised you'
  • 'Write: I used to think ___, but now I think ___'
  • 'Draw a time this week when you felt a strong feeling. What was it? What caused it?'
  • 'Did you change your mind about anything this week? Draw it or write it.'

Changing your mind is a sign of thinking, not weakness. This is stated explicitly to students in Grade 1 — and repeated every year through Grade 12.

What Grade 1 Builds

Ready for Grade 2

  • 1
    Can state a claim with a reason and point to evidence'I think X because Y, and I know this because Z' — said correctly and consistently.
  • 2
    Can identify cause and effect in stories and real eventsAnd can articulate the direction: because points backward, so points forward.
  • 3
    Can complete and extend if/then rulesConditional logic in natural language, applied to daily situations.
  • 4
    Can draw a simple argument map with three componentsClaim box, reason box, evidence box — connected with labeled arrows.
  • 5
    Maintains a Thinking Journal without promptingThe habit of noticing and recording what changes in their thinking is established.
  • 6
    Recognizes when an argument is missing its reason'She made a claim but didn't say why' — gap detection in its earliest form.
