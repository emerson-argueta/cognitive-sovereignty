Core Principle

The Curriculum's Spine

Every citizen — regardless of whether they become an analyst, a farmer, an electrician, a surgeon, or a software engineer — will spend their life navigating an information environment engineered to manipulate them.

The curriculum recognizes that logic cannot function in a state of physiological hijack. Therefore, the ability of a 5-year-old to name a feeling is the direct prerequisite for an 18-year-old to dismantle a sophisticated disinformation campaign. We build the Internal Heatmap first so the logic gate can remain functional under pressure.

This vertical thread — from emotional labeling in Pre-K to inoculation exercises in 8th grade to self-directed civic reasoning in 12th grade — is not incidental to the curriculum. It is the curriculum's spine.

Changing your mind is treated, from the first day of school, as a sign of thinking — not weakness.

I
Phase One · Pre-K to 5th Grade Becoming a person who can learn

No curriculum can reach a child who cannot yet regulate frustration, sit with uncertainty, or ask "why?" without being made to feel that the question is rude. Phase One builds the human foundations on which all future learning rests.

There are no tracks in Phase One. Every child does everything. Specialization has no place here.

Phase One · Pre-K & Kindergarten

The Embodied Foundation

Learning at this stage is almost entirely physical and emotional.

Emotional Development

  • Naming feelings accurately: anger, frustration, excitement, fear, confusion
  • Learning that a feeling is information, not a command
  • The earliest Internal Heatmap: "I notice I feel upset. That is a signal, not a verdict."
  • Conflict resolution through words rather than physical reaction

Physical Development

  • Fine and gross motor skills through structured play and creative building
  • Spatial reasoning through block construction, drawing, and physical puzzles
  • Body awareness: the body responds before the mind catches up

Early Pattern Recognition

  • Sorting, sequencing, and categorizing through play
  • Simple cause-and-effect: "If I do this, then that happens"
  • Story structure: beginning, middle, end — the earliest argument map

Curiosity as a Habit

  • Teachers model asking "why?" and "how do we know?"
  • No question is treated as disruptive
  • Wonder is practiced, not just permitted
Phase One · Grades 1 & 2

Reasoning Through Story and Pattern

Connecting emotional awareness to basic logical structure through narrative and play.

Reasoning & Language

  • Cause and effect in stories and real events
  • The difference between "because" and "so": directional logic
  • Simple claim and evidence: "I think X because Y"
  • Recognizing when a story "doesn't make sense" and articulating why

Emotional Logic

  • Connecting feelings to decisions: "She did that because she felt..."
  • The same event can make different people feel different things
  • Perspective as a logical variable

Pattern Recognition

  • Numerical and visual patterns
  • Sequences with missing elements
  • If/then thinking through games and puzzles
Phase One · Grades 3 & 4

The Bridge to Structured Thinking

Abstract thinking begins to emerge. Critical inquiry is introduced explicitly for the first time.

Structured Reasoning

  • Claim → Reason → Evidence structure introduced
  • Identifying opinions versus facts in texts and conversations
  • "How do we know that?" practiced as a habit, not a challenge
  • Simple fallacy recognition: "everyone says so" examined as a reason

Pattern Recognition & Early Logic

  • Boolean thinking through games: AND / OR / NOT rules
  • Identifying patterns in data presented visually
  • Algorithmic thinking through step-by-step process mapping

Emotional Development

  • Recognizing emotional manipulation: peer pressure, advertising aimed at children
  • Internal Heatmap introduced explicitly: "When you feel a strong pull, that is a signal worth examining"
  • Distinguishing useful emotional information from deliberately triggered responses
Phase One · Grade 5

The Transition Year

By the end of 5th grade, every student asks "how do we know that?" without hesitation or apology.

Reasoning & Argument

  • Full Claim → Reason → Evidence → Counterargument structure
  • Strong vs. weak argument identification
  • Source evaluation: who wrote this, why, and for whom?
  • Media literacy: presentation choices carry meaning

Metacognition Introduced

  • The Thinking Journal begins: what did I believe before, what do I believe now, what changed?
  • Updating a belief based on evidence, practiced without shame
  • Changing your mind is a sign of thinking, not weakness

Portfolio Foundation

  • Students collect work that shows their thinking — not just correct answers
  • Development Key concept introduced: your thinking process is private; what you demonstrate is shared
II
Phase Two · 6th to 8th Grade Discovering what kinds of thinking feel alive

The middle school years serve one overarching purpose: giving students a wide enough range of intellectual experiences that they begin to discover where they come alive. The competency tracks introduced here are not assignments — they are orientations students move between freely.

The Curriculum Orchestrator tracks not just competency completion but engagement signals: where does a student linger, ask unsolicited questions, or produce work beyond the requirement? These signals surface as invitations for conversation, not data for sorting.

Phase Two · Grade 6

Foundations

Building the vocabulary of argument and the practice of self-observation.

Core Skills

  • Basic pattern recognition and text structure analysis
  • Common informal fallacies: Ad Hominem, Strawman, False Dichotomy, Appeal to Authority
  • Internal Heatmap: mapping physiological responses to charged information
  • Mind maps and simple argument diagrams as thinking tools
  • The Thinking Journal continued and deepened
Phase Two · Grade 7

Structure

Moving from recognition to mapping, evaluation, and the metacognitive layer.

Core Skills

  • Formal argument mapping: the Toulmin Model — Claim → Grounds → Warrant → Rebuttal
  • Boolean logic: AND / OR / NOT as decision and analysis tools
  • Source credibility evaluation and media framing analysis
  • Toulmin Flowcharts and Boolean logic gate diagrams

Metacognitive Layer (introduced this year)

  • Students formally learn to map their own emotional responses before engaging analytically
  • Introduction to Red Team exercises with full Socratic Debrief Protocol
  • The Internal Heatmap evolves from observation to active analysis tool
Phase Two · Grade 8

Synthesis

Research-level argumentation. Scientific skepticism. Formal defense.

Core Skills

  • Predicate Logic: recognizing quantifiers (∀, ∃) in natural language and formal contexts
  • Scientific skepticism: statistical fallacies, cherry-picked data, p-hacking, motivated reasoning
  • Construction of formal arguments and structured rebuttals
  • Full Toulmin Flowcharts with rebuttal branches; heatmaps as evidence tools

Capstone Defense

  • A formal presentation of an argument, a dismantled claim, or a designed system
  • Advanced Red Team / Inoculation exercises
  • Students defend their reasoning before peers and a panel
Phase Two · Structural Model

Interdisciplinary Deep Work

8:00 AM – 1:00 PM. One Core Problem. Five lenses. Zero homework.

Each week is anchored by a Core Problem — not a logical theme — explored through five lenses simultaneously. The day alternates 35 minutes of focused instruction with 15-minute Brain Breaks.

LensDescription
Reading & WritingAnalyzing bias in real-world texts; technical writing with precise claim structure
Pattern RecognitionFrom numerical patterns to algorithms and formal logic
VisualizationTranslating abstract arguments into flowcharts, heatmaps, and logic gate diagrams
Project-Based LearningApplying the week's tools to a tangible, assessable outcome
Metacognitive ReflectionJournaling emotional and physiological responses to the week's material

Example Core Problem: "The Resource Allocation Problem"
Boolean If/Then rules define distribution logic → A heatmap visualizes where resources concentrate → Students design a board game using those rules → A real government budget is analyzed for framing → Metacognitive reflection asks: which allocation felt "unfair," and why?

Phase Two · Assessment Model

Branching Competency Model

Three orientations. Fluid movement. No stigma.

Core Competency — All Students

  • The minimum viable skill set for information defense in any life path
  • At 8th grade: understanding the difference between "some studies show" and "all evidence confirms"

Extension Competency — For Students Who Have Mastered Core

  • Formal proofs, structured academic argument, mathematical logic
  • For students moving toward university-track academic work

Vocational Depth Track

  • Argument mapping applied to contracts and materials safety data sheets
  • Boolean logic applied to irrigation, electrical routing, or construction sequencing
  • Evaluating manufacturer claims as statistical arguments
  • Spotting predatory pricing using pattern recognition

Design principle: The Vocational Depth Track is not remedial. It treats tradespeople and laborers as the population most exposed to predatory information — because they often are, and with the least institutional protection. The same Orchestrator, the same Five Lenses, and the same assessment standards apply across all tracks. Parity of esteem is structural, not rhetorical.

Phase Two · Defensive Literacy

Red Team Inoculation Exercises

Understanding the craft of manipulation provides a more durable defense than labeling it.

Students construct — but never publish — a piece of persuasive misinformation. Framed not as "how to lie" but as "mapping the vulnerabilities in human cognition." Grounded in inoculation theory research (van der Linden et al.).

These exercises must be led by a Certified Facilitator. This is a specialized competency, not a general teaching skill.

The Socratic Debrief Protocol — Three Required Steps

  • The Anchor of Truth: Students dismantle the misinformation they just created using their own logic tools
  • The Source of Authority: Discussion reframes around interoception — which emotional lever did you pull, and why does it work?
  • The Why of Skepticism: Skepticism is the tool we use to protect the possibility of truth, not to destroy it

A poorly facilitated debrief does not produce a neutral outcome. It produces cynicism or nihilism — specific harms that are worse than not running the exercise at all. This protocol is non-negotiable.

III
Phase Three · 9th to 12th Grade Going deep in the direction of your calling

High school is where the tools get applied to students' own chosen domains with genuine stakes and genuine autonomy. The tracks diverge in intensity — but never completely. Every student maintains fluency in all tracks while pursuing depth in one or two.

No student fully commits to a single track before the end of 10th grade. Deep specialization begins in 11th grade. Track movement requires nothing more than a conversation and a willingness to try. No qualifying tests. No formal re-enrollment.

Phase Three · Structure

The Studio Model

Distinguishing compelled labor from chosen labor.

8:00 AM – 1:00 PM · Core Instruction Block

  • Mandatory logic instruction, seminars, and guided deep work
  • The 35/15 rhythm continues
  • All required work is completed here — Zero Homework honored

1:00 PM – 5:00 PM · The Resourced Studio

  • Facilities remain open as mentored project spaces
  • Staff present in a non-instructional capacity — available but not directing
  • Attendance is entirely voluntary
  • Technical assistance and safety oversight provided

A student who stays until 5:00 PM because they cannot put down what they are building is developing genuine intrinsic motivation — the cognitive signature of a person who has found their calling. A student who leaves at 1:00 PM has met every requirement. Both outcomes are valid.

Phase Three · Track One

The Academic Track

For students called toward the construction and contestation of knowledge.

9th–10th Grade · Breadth

  • Philosophy of science: how knowledge is built, tested, and overturned
  • Formal logic and proof theory
  • Rhetoric and the history of argument
  • Research methodology: study design, peer review, replication

11th–12th Grade · Depth

  • Epistemology: what can we know, and how do we know we know it?
  • Advanced formal logic and symbolic reasoning
  • Original research projects with formal defense
  • Engagement with genuine academic literature and real unsolved questions
  • Capstone: A formal written argument or research paper defended before a panel
Phase Three · Track Two

The Vocational Track

For students called toward skilled trades, agriculture, and physical craftsmanship.

9th–10th Grade · Breadth

  • Applied logic in real trade contexts: contracts, specifications, safety documentation
  • Financial literacy: predatory terms, loans, pricing structures
  • Introduction to apprenticeship contexts with real mentors
  • Systems thinking: how a building, farm, or machine works as an integrated system

11th–12th Grade · Depth

  • Active apprenticeship with genuine stakes — real projects, real clients, real consequences
  • Advanced contract and regulatory literacy
  • Business reasoning: cost analysis, risk evaluation, resource allocation
  • Capstone: A real project with full documentation of every decision made and why
Phase Three · Track Three

Systems & Engineering Track

For students called toward building things that work — the doers who are also thinkers.

9th–10th Grade · Breadth

  • Systems modeling: understanding how complex systems fail and why
  • Design under constraint: iterative building, prototyping, formal failure analysis
  • Computational thinking: algorithms, data structures, the logic of machines
  • Introduction to engineering disciplines through hands-on projects

11th–12th Grade · Depth

  • Advanced design projects with formal constraint documentation
  • Failure analysis: students study real engineering failures and reconstruct the reasoning errors that caused them
  • Cross-disciplinary systems thinking: agricultural, civic, and software systems analyzed with the same framework
  • Capstone: Design, build, and formally defend something that works — every decision and every failure documented honestly

The Studio Model is particularly vital for this track. Iteration requires unstructured time. A student debugging a structural failure at 3:00 PM is doing exactly the work this track is designed to produce.

Phase Three · Track Four

Civic & Ethical Track

A thread woven through all tracks, with its own depth for those called to it.

This track is not fully separable from the others — every student engages with civic and ethical reasoning throughout high school. For students called toward law, policy, social work, or public service, it offers its own depth.

9th–10th Grade · Breadth (All Students)

  • Policy reasoning: evaluating proposals using logic tools, not tribal affiliation
  • Institutional analysis: how governments, courts, corporations, and media distort incentives
  • Moral philosophy introduced as a set of rigorous questions, not a set of answers
  • Democratic participation: students engage with a real local civic issue through to formal presentation

11th–12th Grade · Depth (Students Pursuing This Track)

  • Advanced moral philosophy and applied ethics
  • Legal reasoning: reading case law, identifying precedent, constructing legal arguments
  • Policy design: develop a real proposal, defend it before critics, revise under pressure
  • Capstone: A formal civic project presented to a real external audience

Systems Layer

The Curriculum Orchestrator

An active curriculum management tool — not a ledger.

Core Functions

  • Provides teachers with Pre-Linked Modules — ready-made connections between lenses for each Core Problem week
  • Tracks competency progress and surfaces engagement signals as invitations for conversation, not data for sorting
  • Flags students who may benefit from track exploration or lateral moves
  • Allows teachers to share Fallacy Logs, debrief notes, and module adaptations across the network
  • Scales from teacher-directed (Phase One) to student-visible and self-directed (Phase Three)
Systems Layer

The Two-Keys Portfolio

Students have the right to fail and grow in private.

🔒 Development Key — Private

  • All process data: drafts, failed proofs, fallacy logs, Red Team exercises, metacognitive journals
  • Stored in a local encrypted container accessible only to student and teacher
  • Automatically purged at end of year unless the student explicitly retains an artifact
  • Zero links to the Credential Key
  • The specific date of mastery is retained here for the student's own tracking only

🔑 Credential Key — Verifiable & Portable

  • Generated when a teacher marks a competency as Mastered
  • Issued in seasonal batches (Summer / Winter) — not the moment mastery is achieved
  • External-facing credential displays Competency Level only — no timestamp visible to universities or employers
  • Zero reference to process data, struggle history, or time-to-mastery
  • Portable for university placement, trade apprenticeships, and vocational programs
  • Built on standard PKI — upgradable to Zero-Knowledge Proof infrastructure as it matures

A university or employer sees the fact of mastery. The rhythm of that mastery — how long it took, how many attempts it required — remains the student's private property.

Implementation

Teacher Training & Certification

Teacher training is not a cost that can be deferred. It is a prerequisite.

General Training — All Staff

  • Phase One: emotional development facilitation; play-based learning pedagogy; metacognitive coaching
  • Branching Track Management: running multiple competency tracks simultaneously
  • Engagement Signal Reading: recognizing when a student may benefit from track exploration
  • Curriculum Orchestrator Operation: modules, competency tracking, lateral moves
  • High School Advising: mentorship rather than instruction for Grades 11–12

Certified Facilitator Status — Red Team Modules Only

  • A renewable professional credential, not a one-time qualification
  • Requires completion of a dedicated Socratic Debrief Protocol training pathway
  • Observed practice sessions evaluated by an existing Certified Facilitator
  • Ongoing peer review of debrief quality
  • Renewal on a defined cycle — the skills are perishable

Network Support for Smaller Schools: A Rotational Facilitator model ensures Red Team exercises are always led by a qualified expert regardless of school size. Remote facilitation protocols are available where in-person rotation is not feasible.

Implementation

Phased Adoption

No school starts K–12 from zero. This is the on-ramp.

A school can begin Stage 1 tomorrow. Stages 2 and 3 require commitment and resources — but by the time a school reaches them, staff and students will have already experienced the value of what came before.

1

The Orchestrator

Teachers begin using the Curriculum Orchestrator to align existing subjects around Core Problems. The Five Lenses are introduced as a planning framework. No changes to grading, schedules, or academic requirements.

GOAL — Build teacher fluency with interdisciplinary problem framing before any structural changes are made
2

The Heatmap & Rhythm

The Internal Heatmap is introduced. The 35/15 focus-break rhythm is adopted. Metacognitive journaling begins. Teacher training for emotional development facilitation is completed. Runs in parallel with the old assessment system.

GOAL — Build the regulatory floor without disrupting existing academic requirements
3

The Ledger & Tracks

The school transitions to the Two-Keys Portfolio system and Branching Mastery logic blocks. The Certified Facilitator pathway is activated. Tracks are introduced starting with the grade level where the Orchestrator has been most successfully embedded.

GOAL — Full structural transition to Branching Competency, credential architecture, and Studio Model
Outcomes

What This System Produces

Not a single type of graduate. People who have found their calling — and the tools to protect it.

The Academic

Can construct a formal proof, evaluate a research paper's methodology, and identify the motivated reasoning in their own conclusions.

The Electrician

Can read a contract, spot predatory terms, evaluate a manufacturer's safety claim, and know when they are being lied to.

The Farmer

Can assess a yield study, recognize a motivated source, and make resource decisions under genuine uncertainty.

The Engineer

Can design under constraint, document every failure honestly, and defend every decision to someone who will push back hard.

The Civic Actor

Can engage with a policy argument without tribal reflex, understand institutional incentives, and participate in democracy as a thinker rather than a reaction.
