Phase One · Grade 5

The Bridge Year

Everything built in Grades 1–4 is assembled into one coherent toolkit.

Grade 5 is where the pieces come together. The claim-reason-evidence structure from Grade 1, the source evaluation from Grade 3, the counterargument from Grade 4, the Boolean logic from Grades 2 and 3 — all of these are unified into the beginning of the Toulmin Model.

This is also the year the Two-Keys Portfolio architecture is introduced in age-appropriate terms. Students learn that their thinking process is private (Development Key) and that what they can demonstrate is what gets shared (Credential Key). The right to fail and grow in private is named explicitly for the first time.

Core Domain · Logic

The Toulmin Model — Introduction

The argument structure that Grade 7 will formalize — introduced here in plain language.

What Students Practice

  • Claim: The position you are defending
  • Grounds: The evidence that supports the claim
  • Warrant: The reasoning that connects the evidence to the claim — the 'because this matters' layer
  • Rebuttal: The strongest objection — and your response to it
  • Drawing a simple 4-box Toulmin diagram for a familiar argument
  • Identifying which box is missing from a given argument — the weakest link

The warrant is the hardest part. Students often give a claim and evidence but skip the warrant — the explanation of why this evidence supports this claim. Finding the missing warrant is the most important analytical skill Grade 5 develops.

Core Domain · Research

Basic Research Protocol

Finding sources, evaluating them, and citing them — the first formal research process.

What Students Practice

  • Finding three sources on a topic and evaluating each using the three-question method from Grade 3
  • Identifying primary vs secondary sources and knowing when each is appropriate
  • Paraphrasing vs quoting — using someone else's idea without stealing their words
  • Simple citation: author, title, where it appeared, when
  • Triangulation: if two independent sources say the same thing, the claim is stronger
Core Domain · Media Literacy

How Presentation Creates Meaning

The same facts, presented differently, produce different beliefs.

What Students Practice

  • Headline framing: comparing headlines about the same event from different outlets
  • Photo selection: which image was chosen to accompany a story, and what impression does it create?
  • What is omitted: the information that isn't in the story — and why it might matter
  • The question: 'What do they want me to feel?' asked about every media artifact encountered
Core Domain · Fallacies

Six Fallacies Mastered

Appeal to Popularity, Ad Hominem, Strawman, False Dichotomy, Slippery Slope, Anecdotal Evidence.

All six fallacies from the Phase One sequence are now practiced in combination. Grade 5 introduces fallacy stacking — arguments that use multiple fallacies simultaneously. Students learn to identify all the problems in a single flawed argument.

New Practice This Year

  • Fallacy stacking: finding multiple fallacies in a single argument
  • Fallacy Log: students document real-world examples they encounter during the year
  • Distinguishing legitimate uses of emotion in argument (pathos) from manipulative ones
The Two-Keys System

Portfolio Foundation

The right to fail and grow in private — introduced in plain language.

What Students Learn

  • Development Key (private): 'Your thinking process — your drafts, your failed attempts, your journal — belongs to you. It is private.'
  • Credential Key (public): 'What you can demonstrate is what gets shared. Not how long it took.'
  • Students begin curating a portfolio: not just correct work, but work that shows their thinking process
  • The capstone defense: presenting and explaining their best argument from the year to an audience
  • 'Changing your mind is a sign of thinking.' The Thinking Journal is reviewed and the student reflects on what changed across the year.

This is the last year without tracks. From Grade 6 onward, students begin discovering which kinds of thinking feel most alive to them. Grade 5 ensures every student arrives at that exploration with the same foundational toolkit.

What Grade 5 Builds

Ready for Phase Two

  • 1
    Can construct and explain a 4-part Toulmin argumentClaim, Grounds, Warrant, Rebuttal — each component identified and defended.
  • 2
    Can conduct basic research using three sourcesFind, evaluate, paraphrase, cite — a complete research micro-cycle.
  • 3
    Recognizes six fallacies and can find multiple in one argumentIncluding fallacy stacking — arguments with multiple simultaneous flaws.
  • 4
    Can analyze a media artifact for framing and omission'What do they want me to feel?' applied consistently to headlines, photos, and stories.
  • 5
    Understands and accepts the Two-Keys Portfolio modelThe distinction between private thinking process and public demonstrated mastery.
  • 6
    Arrives at Grade 6 with the full Phase One toolkitSix years of emotional regulation, pattern logic, argument structure, source evaluation, and visual reasoning — ready to be formalized.
